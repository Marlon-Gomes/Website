      About the seminar

      Our goal is to bring together students interested in geometry at Stony Brook to discuss topics in the interface between symplectic, algebraic, and differential geometry.
      Our activities are part of the NSF-supported joint Math/SCGP training program in geometry, which you can learn more about here.
      Previous topics included: The Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence; The moduli of J-holomorphic curves; Mirror symmetry for the quintic.
      The seminar is jointly organized by  Marlon de Oliveira Gomes and John Sheridan.

      Spring 2018

      The topic for this spring is Stability Conditions and Wall-crossing Phenomena. Below is a summary of our goals for the semester.

      Classification of geometric objects (manifolds, varieties, bundles, sheaves) is an important problem in geometry. In simple situations, e.g., diffeomorphism classification of closed (compact without boundary), orientable surfaces, the classification scheme is discrete: there is a countable collection of invariants capable of distinguishing the objects in question. More refined problems, such as classifying complex structures on a closed, orientable surface of genus greater than 0 require a continuum of parameters, thus leading to the notion of moduli spaces.

      These moduli spaces themselves are often interesting geometric objects. However, they are not always easy to deal with: in certain situation, the moduli space can exhibit pathologies, such as non-Hausdorff topologies, failure of compactness, and so on. A situation we will explore in this seminar is the construction of a moduli space of vector bundles on an algebraic curve. The fundamental observation is that a generic vector bundle (or more generally, coherent sheaf) can be understood in terms of simpler objects, called stable bundles. In turn, the set of stable bundles has a nice structure (e.g. it has a natural Hausdorff topology), which one can explore. We will devote the first part of the seminar to understand concretely this construction from both the algebraic and symplectic viewpoints.

      In certain problems in geometry (such as the classification of bundles on higher-dimensional varieties) there are various non-equivalent notions of stability, sometimes even a continuum of such notions. It is natural to ask how do the moduli of stable objects vary under a change in the stability condition. In recent years there have been several attempts to try to understand this problem. One of them, due to Tom Bridgeland, will be the focus of the second part of our seminar. We will describe the notion of an abstract stability condition on variety, and the structure of the set of such conditions. In Bridgeland's theory, the set of stability conditions forms a complex manifold. In certain situations, this manifold admits a wall-and-chamber structure: there are real, codimension 1 submanifolds, called walls, dividing the variety of stability conditions into chambers (the connected components of the complement of the set of walls); the moduli spaces of two stability conditions in the same chamber are similar, in a appropriate sense, while the moduli spaces of stability conditions in different chambers are different. We want to understand the wall-crossing phenomena, i.e., the transformations the moduli spaces undergo under wall-crossing, and how these transformations affect the invariants of the moduli space that one is interested in. We will try to keep the discussion as concrete as possible by studying examples of such phenomena in classical theory, such as variation of GIT quotients and changes in polarization for slope-stability.
